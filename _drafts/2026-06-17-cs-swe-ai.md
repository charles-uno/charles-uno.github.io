Computers are impossibly good at math. They crunch numbers a million times faster, cheaper, and more accurately than any human. This creates a sizable incentive to turn all sorts of problems into math problems. As soon as you've turned something into a math problem, you can outsource it to a computer, which will solve it quickly and reliably.

That's what software engineers do. They take a problem, break it into computer-friendly pieces, and plug it into a computer using programming code. Software engineering is a well-paid profession that has seen consistent growth over the past few decades. Many college students study computer science with the intention of becoming software engineers. 

Recent advances in AI tooling have thrown the software engineering industry for a loop. AI agents are good at writing code now. Experienced engineers can use AI tools to work faster. Non-engineers can use these tools to "[vibecode][vibecode]" software with minimal knowledge of the underlying machine. CEOs are eager to save money by [cutting employees][block_layoffs] in favor of AI. Computer science educators are scrambling to adjust. 

[vibecode]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibe_coding
[block_layoffs]: https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/26/business/block-layoffs-ai-jack-dorsey

I don't know the future, of course, but I've got a pretty good view of the present. I've been a software engineer for ten years. The past five have been in big tech. I also moonlight as a computer science professor. I have grudgingly become a regular AI user for both coding and curriculum development. Here's how things look from where I'm standing.

## Demand for Coding Skills Peaked with the Pandemic

Over the course of the 2010s, [software ate the world][software_ate_the_world]. Tech companies hired aggressively as they expanded into brick-and-mortar markets. For example:

- Netflix [bankrupted Blockbuster][netflix_blockbuster] in 2010
- By 2014, more people got their [news from Facebook][fb_overtakes_newspapers] than from newspapers
- Rideshare apps [surpassed traditional taxis][uber_taxis] around 2015
- Music streaming apps [overtook music purchases][spotify_cds] around 2016

Then came the COVID pandemic. People were home all day glued to their smartphones. This was more money in tech company pockets, which further fueled the demand for software engineers:

- [Ad spending on social media][covid_online_ads] saw double-digit growth at the expense of print media
- App-based food delivery [tripled][covid_food_delivery]
- Video call apps saw usage increase [*thirty*-fold][covid_video_calls]. 

There were only so many software engineers to go around. Companies competed for them. Salaries and [perks][perks] went up. Hiring standards went down. I was lucky enough to experience this firsthand. A recruiter from Meta called me in late 2020. My resume was decent. My interview went badly. They ghosted me. But then, a few weeks later, their hiring quota went up. They called me back with an offer to double my salary.

College students took notice. They learned to code. From 2010 to 2024, the number of students graduating with a computer science major [increased five-fold][fivefold_increase]. The total number of software engineers in the USA, in turn, went from [one million in 2010][2010_swe_count] to [nearly two million in 2024][2024_swe_count]. 

But as the pandemic wound down, so did the hiring frenzy. Software was already integrated into every part of daily life, so there was no more room to expand. Job postings have been [trending down][swe_jobs_dropping_post_covid] for the past few years, with entry-level positions [hit hardest][swe_drop_by_seniority]. Layoffs are [rising][2026_tech_layoffs]. Salaries for new computer science grads are [dropping][swe_salary_dropping] closer to the other sciences. The pendulum was swinging away from computer science majors even before AI got good.

People often study computer science to get hired as software engineers, but computer science and software engineering aren't quite the same thing:

- Computer science covers the fundamental logic, mathematics, and physics of computing. Computer science students learn how the machine works, algorithms for breaking down complex problems into machine-friendly steps, and programming languages for communicating those steps to the machine. 
- Software engineering is about delivering a product. Messaging platforms, food delivery apps, media players, operating systems, mobile games, etc. The user experience is what's important. The computer is just a tool along the way.

Your average software engineer doesn't need to worry about how compilers work. Your average computer science grads doesn't really understand tech debt. Ten years ago, that was fine. The job market was ferociously hungry for coding skills. Computer science grads had those skills. So we ended up with a pipeline from computer science to software engineering.

Coding skills are less important now. There's little need to speak the computer's language when AI allows the machine to (more or less) understand English and Chinese. My peers and I rarely write code by hand anymore. We describe our requirements and let the AI work out the details. Software engineering just doesn't overlap that much with computer science anymore:

![Venn diagram comparing computer science and software engineering](/files/cs-swe-ai/swe-venn-3-way.png)

To be clear: software engineering is still a technical job! Software engineers just act more like tech leads now, rather than individual contributors. For example:

- We spend a lot of time breaking down big goals into little tasks
- We check the AI output for mistakes, then tweak the environment to help it do better next time
- We keep track of how our product fits into the bigger system
- We set priorities and allocate resources

These are traditionally senior-level skills, but they're now [being asked of junior engineers][senior_skills] too. That's not good! Expereince matters, and, by definition, a kid fresh out of school doesn't have much of it. As educators, we can do our best to teach judgment and leadership. As an industry, we *must* ensure there's a pathway for today's junior engineers to become tomorrow's senior engineers.

## AI Won't Stop at Software Engineering

Software engineers do more than code. We have countless other fiddly little tasks throughout the day, such as:

- Turning a few important bullet points into a presentable document
- Parsing project timelines from half a dozen different docs into a flowchart
- Finding a "good enough" answer to a vague question
- Cleaning up an outdated wiki page
- Taking a bunch of old data and converting it to a more convenient format

<!--
- Summarizing a pile of emails after a week of vacation
- Building a dashboard to show metrics that only matter to a handful of people
-->

These tasks do not require any particular creativity, judgment, or other human touch. We do them manually is because we don't have software to do them automatically, and they're too specific to justify writing new code. This is the sort of task we're happy to delegate to a junior engineer or an intern. 

AI is increasingly doing these fiddly little tasks. Sometimes it shoehorns the task into an existing tool. Sometimes it writes new code on the fly. Either way, the task gets done faster than I would have done it by hand. In other words, AI has significantly decreased the cost of outsourcing tasks to the machine. 

As far as I can tell, other industries are *not* seeing AI absorb their fiddly little tasks, at least not to the same degree. Software engineering was hit first. This makes sense for a few reasons:

- Big tech is investing a lot of money in AI tooling. We have whole teams of engineers figuring out how to properly plug AI into our internal systems. When I ask it to do something, it generally just *works*. Our in-house tooling is a step or two ahead of what you'd see from that same model fresh out of the box. 
- Pretty much every tool used we use has a text interface via the Linux terminal. This makes it easy for us to write little scripts to automate the tedious parts of our jobs. It's also a convenient interface for AI tooling. Buttons and dropdowns and other "pretty" interfaces are trickier to use programmatically.
- AI can respond very differently depending on how a prompt is phrased. Software engineers are very good at breaking tasks down into computer-friendly pieces.
- Software engineers are expensive! Your average software engineer at Meta costs the company over $100/hour. For that price, Zuck wants to squeeze every drop of expertise out of us. He does not want us to spend time copy-pasting numbers from Word into PowerPoint.

There are plenty of interns in finance, retail, architecture, and a hundred other industries. I presume those industries also have lots of fiddly tasks that could be delegated to AI agents. The transition will happen as soon as it's cost-effective to do so.

Some of my non-software-engineer friends have started using AI at work. Many more are being pressed to do so, but find it unhelpful. 


Other industries will catch up. Wrappers and integration points will pop up to allow AI to interface nicely with proprietary software and data formats. Job responsibilities will change as we find that AI is undeniably better at some sorts of work. This will be a mix of good and bad, just as we've seen in software engineering. Outsourcing some work to AI will allow people to focus more on what they want to *do* and less on the tooling necessary to do it. The transition will also be a huge pain in the ass that leaves some folks behind.

## Abstinence-Only Education Doesn't Work

Researchers in China recently [published a study][china_ai_study] looking at how AI use impacted school performance. The top-line numbers are about what you would expect: the average student is getting better scores on homework, spending less time on it, and performing worse on tests. In other words, they're outsourcing their homework to AI, engaging less with the material, and learning less themselves. 

But some students defied the average. Some AI users spent just as much time on homework as their non-AI-user peers. These students say their test scores *increase* instead of decrease. The idea is that those students are using AI as a tutor, rather than as a machine that spits out the answers.

[china_ai_study]: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6868618

This aligns pretty closely with my experience using AI for curriculum development. AI can write slides just fine, but if I show up to class with AI-written slides I'm going to have a bad time. The voice will be off, emphasis might not line up with my priorities, and it might even bring up a topic I'm not prepared to talk about. But AI is an incredible resource for exploring new material. It can find answers even when I'm not sure exactly what I'm looking for. Chatting with an agent is both faster and more engaging than reading Wikipedia pages from top to bottom. For example:

- "I'm developing a lecture about the TCP/IP model of networking for an undergraduate computer science course. I'd like to talk about security. Can you list some important network vulnerabilities for each layer?"
- "My chapter on CPU architecture is feeling a bit light. We already cover the ALU, instruction pipelining, and hazards. Could you suggest some other topics I could add to flesh it out?"
- lorem ipsum...

The study above suggests that some students intuitively use AI like this. Maybe more will adopt this approach as they gain experience. But I also believe we should be teaching this skill. Students have access to AI regardless. A decent fraction of them will use it whether we want them to or not. We should coach them to use it as a learning resource rather than as an answer machine.

We can also talk about the fact that AI allows students to write *more* code and also write it *faster*. This can be an opportunity to assign more ambitious -- and, dare I say, more interesting -- projects. 

We lose the excitement of writing code with our own hands. But we gain the excitement of writing code that is more exciting than `print("Hello World!")`

- Gell-Mann amnesia effect
- LaTeX. If you want it to look "your way" then you're going to have a fight. You have to be willing to take your hands off the wheel
- There are two data structures: array and hash table (on contiguous memory). Memory is too slow to chase pointers
- We have to do away with "the bogus notion that there is a correct answer to this problem"
