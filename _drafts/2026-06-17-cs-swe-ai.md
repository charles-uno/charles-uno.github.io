When I graduated from St Olaf College in 2009, we had about a dozen computer science majors per year. When I came back to teach, that number was pushing sixty. The trend looks similar at the national level. The number of students majoring in computer science [increased by a factor of five][fivefold_increase] in the 2010s. The software engineering profession [nearly][2010_swe_count] [doubled][2020_swe_count] in size during that same time.

Lots of students majored in computer science to meet the steep demand for coding skills. Companies were scrambling to hire software engineers as [software ate the world][software_ate_the_world]. On top of that, they were trying to hire another layer of software engineers to implement the [internal infrastructure and processes][history_of_devops] popularized by big tech. Not to mention the demand added during COVID, when the whole world was suddenly online. Job seekers with the ability to write code -- computer science graduates and [bootcampers][bootcamp] alike -- saw strong salaries and extravagant benefits.

The thing is, computer science and software engineering are pretty different (see diagram below). Computer science covers the logical, mathematical, and physical foundations of computing. Software engineering, on the other hand, is about delivering a product. Only [about 15%][swe_breakdown] of software engineers engage meaningfully with the machine. That 15% then builds infrastructure so that the other 85% can take the machine for granted and focus on the product. 

![Venn diagram comparing computer science and software engineering](/files/cs-swe-ai/swe-venn-2010s.png)
*Computer science students spend a decent amount of time learning to code. Those skills were in high demand throughout the 2010s.*

Ten years ago, the industry was so hungry for coding skills that the difference between computer science and software engineering didn't much matter. Now the winds are shifting. Job postings have been [trending down][swe_jobs_dropping_post_covid] since the COVID-era peak. Layoffs are [rising][2026_tech_layoffs]. Salaries are [dropping][swe_salary_dropping]. And on top of all that, recent improvements in AI tooling have fundamentally changed what software engineering looks like as a profession. Coding has almost entirely been delegated to AI agents at this point. Algorithms and programming syntax are no longer important skills for humans. Without those skills, software engineering just doesn't overlap much with computer science anymore (see digram below). 

![Venn diagram comparing computer science and software engineering](/files/cs-swe-ai/swe-venn-3-way.png)
*Most coding is now delegated to AI agents. There's not much overlap left between computer science and software engineering.*

Alarm bells are ringing in computer science departments. It feels like we've been teaching long division and they just invented the pocket calculator. 

I don't know the future, but I do have a pretty good view of the present. I've been a software engineer for ten years. The last five have been in big tech. I also moonlight as a computer science professor. Six months ago I was ambivalent to AI (or even annoyed by it); now I'm a daily user. Here's how things look from where I'm standing. 

## AI Is Bigger Than Software Engineering

In a big-picture sense, my job today is the same it was ten years ago. I'm applying my problem-solving skills, creativity, and judgment to move product goals forward. But my day-to-day workflow looks wildly different:

- I rarely write code by hand anymore. Instead, I describe my requirements and let the AI work through the details.
- I no longer search across wikis and documents to find information. AI can usually pull it up right away.
- Most of my tickets are written by AI. I give it a few bullet points. It fills in the context and relevant links.

It's like having a team of interns who work at superhuman speed. I spend a lot of time allocating work between them, flagging their mistakes, and keeping track of how all the individual projects fit together. A year ago, I was a hands-on engineer. Now I'm more like a project manager.

I suspect we'll see a similar pattern in other industries as well. There are plenty of interns in finance, retail, architecture, and a hundred other industries. That means there are plenty of low-level tasks that can be delegated to AI agents. Software engineering was just the first industry to be affected, which, honestly, makes perfect sense:

- Tech companies had a head start. For example, AI is closely related to natural language processing (used for Siri) and machine learning (used for suggested content on social media). 
- Software engineering has tools to mitigate non-deterministic behavior. An AI agent can run static code analysis on its own output, see the mistakes, and iterate on them. 
- Programming code is consumed by machines. As much as it pains me to say so, there is generally no punishment for ugly code, and generally no reward for beautiful code. 
- Software engineers love building scripts that automate the tedious parts of our own jobs. Pretty much everything we do is accessible via the Linux shell. Plug the AI agent into the Linux shell and it's good to go. 

other industries will catch up. they'll build their own wrappers around the AI to help the agents "understand" the use case. they'll bridge the gap between AI agents and obscure proprietary interfaces.

## Abstinence-Only Education Won't Work

Students have easy access to AI. If they view it as a tool for *producing answers*, they can easily short-circuit their own learning. 

To paraphrase [Titus Winters][tw]: I'm not assigning this because I want to read another essay about Lord of the Flies. I'm assigning it in the hope that you'll learn while doing it. 

[tw]: https://speakers.acm.org/speakers/winters_20748

that all said:
- I think we should be teaching responsible AI use broadly, not just in computer science departments
- Even if computer science departments focus *heavily* on AI, I don't think we can reclaim the glory of the 2010s

CS *could* go back to being a niche major. There will always be a demand for Linux admins. Someone has to write the compilers and design the chips. I doubt this is what you want, but I do want to call it out as an option.

If we want CS to stay big, the program probably needs to maintain itself as an entrypoint to software engineering. That means leaning into skills that are important to AI-based software engineering, and leaning away from less-relevant fundamentals to make room.

These (in my opinion) are some areas to lean into:

- System design. Big-picture familiarity with the different components in a distributed system, as well as the constraints of thost components
- Verification. Static analysis, test-driven development, and validation criteria. Find problems fast to iterate fast
- Code defense. I don't care where you code came from. I want you to stand up and convince me that it's safe to deploy for a billion users
- DevOps concepts. AI can change thousands of lines of code in an instant. Agile/Scrum for scoping and coordination across a team. Pipelines, containers, and feature flags govern deployment. Telemetry, alerts, and oncall rotations are how you respond to problems that slip through
- Tech debt. Figuring out a legacy code base. Consolidating duplicate logic. Data and logic migrations
- Privacy and security. Responsible handling of user data. Vulnerabilities. Attack vectors
- Practice using AI tools! But in a way that doesn't short-circuit learning

These are some areas to lean away from:

- Specific syntax for different languages. It's ok to let AI and linters handle the brackets and semicolons
- Toy scripts. If AI can do it in a one-sentence prompt, there's not much value in asking a human to write it. Unless it helps solidify the concepts in their brain??
- ??? very hard to know what fundamentals are load-bearing ???

Notes from the TW talk:
- Gell-Mann amnesia effect
- LaTeX. If you want it to look "your way" then you're going to have a fight. You have to be willing to take your hands off the wheel
- There are two data structures: array and hash table (on contiguous memory). Memory is too slow to chase pointers
- We have to do away with "the bogus notion that there is a correct answer to this problem"
- We never cared about the algorithms knowledge from Leetcode problems. it was valuable as a signal for "I have invested extra time in gaining expertise here"
- "We used to assign homework in the hope that it would lead to learning" -- the world does not actually need another essay about lord of the flies
- Concern: how do we make sure we are not throwing students in the deep end? There is some pride that comes with building your first Hello World. Does that carry over if we jump straight into bigger AI-build projects?
- S-type work vs E-type work. Specified vs evolved. School mostly focuses on S-type work. Most "real" work is E-type, where you dig down until you can find some S-type stuff (then build a tool to solve it)
