When I graduated from St Olaf College in 2009, there were about a dozen computer science majors per year. Fifteen years later, I came back to teach. The school overall remained about the same size, but the computer science department was now pumping out *sixty* majors per year. The major became much more popular because coding skills led to lucrative jobs in the fast-growing software engineering industry. 

Now the pendulum is swinging the other way. Recent advances in AI tooling have fundamentally changed what software engineering looks like. Line-by-line coding is mostly delegated to AI agents. Coding skills are much less marketable than they were a few years ago. Computer science educators are scrambling to adjust. 

I don't know the future, of course, but I've got a pretty good view of the present. I've been a software engineer for ten years. The past five have been in big tech. I also moonlight as a computer science professor. At first I dragged my feet on AI at work, but earlier this year it became too impactful to deny. Here's how things look from where I'm standing. 

## Demand for Coding Skills Peaked with the Pandemic

There were fewer than a million software engineers in the US in 2010. By 2020, that number was 1.5 million. The profession grew by more than 50% in a decade.

The growth was largely build by a huge increase in students majoring in computer science. From 2010 to 2024, the number of computer science majors per year increased by a factor of *five*. 

The root cause: software was eating the world. Tech giants got big. Lucrative tech jobs. Salaries. Benefits. Coding bootcamps. 
Ferocious demand for coding skills. Computer science students spend a lot of time coding.
DevOps. Extra layer of software engineers. Everyone wanted to implement the internal infrastructure and processes popularized by big tech. 
COVID pandemic. Everything was suddenly online.

I got hired during covid! My resume was ok, but nothing special. I bombed the interview. The recruiter ghosted me. Then, weeks later, he called me back. I'm guessing the hiring quota went up so they scraped me off the bottom of the barrel.

Now the winds are shifting. Software engineering job postings peaked in 2022 and have been falling since. We've now fallen below the pre-pandemic level. Layoffs are increasing. Salaries are dropping.

## Software Engineering Isn't About Computer Science (Anymore)

Software engineering and computer science have always been different. Computer science covers the fundamental logic, mathematics, and physics of computing. Software engineering is about delivering a product. The computer is just a tool along the way. Only [about 15%][swe_breakdown] of software engineers work on low-level systems where they engage meaningfully with the machine. That 15% then builds infrastructure so the other 85% can take the machine for granted and focus on the product. 

That said, ten years ago, the difference didn't much matter. The job market was ferociously hungry for coding skills. Computer science graduates had coding skills. So we ended up with a pipeline from computer science education to lucrative jobs in software engineering. 

But now AI has fundamentally changed what software engineering looks like. In a big-picture sense, my software engineering job today is about the same as it was ten years ago. I'm applying my problem-solving skills, creativity, and judgment to move product goals forward. But my day-to-day workflow looks wildly different:

- I rarely write code by hand anymore. Instead, I describe my requirements and let the AI work through the details.
- I no longer search across wikis and documents to find information. AI can usually pull it up right away.
- Most of my updates and tickets are written by AI. I give it a few bullet points. It fills in the context and relevant links.

It's like having a team of interns who work at superhuman speed. I spend a lot of time allocating work between them, flagging their mistakes, and keeping track of how all the individual projects fit together. A year ago, I was a hands-on engineer. Now I'm more like a project manager.

## AI Will Redefine Other Fields Too

I suspect we'll see a similar pattern in other industries as well. There are plenty of interns in finance, retail, architecture, and a hundred other industries. That means there are plenty of low-level tasks that can be delegated to AI agents. Software engineering was just the first industry to be affected, which, honestly, makes perfect sense:

- Tech companies had a head start. For example, AI is closely related to natural language processing (used for Siri) and machine learning (used for suggested content on social media). 
- Software engineering has tools to mitigate non-deterministic behavior. An AI agent can run static code analysis on its own output, see the mistakes, and iterate on them independently. 
- Software engineers love writing scripts that automate the tedious parts of our own jobs. Pretty much everything we do is accessible via the Linux terminal. Once we figured out how to connect AI agents to the terminal, we were good to go.
- Programming code is consumed by machines, not humans. As much as it pains me to say so, there is generally no punishment for ugly code, and generally no reward for beautiful code. 

other industries will catch up. they'll build their own wrappers around the AI to help the agents "understand" the use case. they'll bridge the gap between AI agents and obscure proprietary interfaces.

AI and vibe coding allow *way* more people to use code to solve problems without worrying about the machine. This sort of software engineering does not require a background in computer science. Software engineers working at a high level would be better served learning the fundamentals of product management so they can effectively translate domain expertise into product requirements.

## Abstinence-Only Education Doesn't Work

Researchers in China recently [published a study][china_ai_study] looking at how AI use impacted school performance. The top-line numbers are about what you would expect: the average student is getting better scores on homework, spending less time on it, and performing worse on tests. In other words, they're outsourcing their homework to AI, engaging less with the material, and learning less themselves. 

But some students defied the average. Some AI users spent just as much time on homework as their non-AI-user peers. These students say their test scores *increase* instead of decrease. The idea is that those students are using AI as a tutor, rather than as a machine that spits out the answers.

[china_ai_study]: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6868618

This aligns pretty closely with my experience using AI for curriculum development. AI can write slides just fine, but if I show up to class with AI-written slides I'm going to have a bad time. The voice will be off, emphasis might not line up with my priorities, and it might even bring up a topic I'm not prepared to talk about. But AI is an incredible resource for exploring new material. It can find answers even when I'm not sure exactly what I'm looking for. Chatting with an agent is both faster and more engaging than reading Wikipedia pages from top to bottom. For example:

- "I'm developing a lecture about the TCP/IP model of networking for an undergraduate computer science course. I'd like to talk about security. Can you list some important network vulnerabilities for each layer?"
- "My chapter on CPU architecture is feeling a bit light. We already cover the ALU, instruction pipelining, and hazards. Could you suggest some other topics I could add to flesh it out?"
- lorem ipsum...

The study above suggests that some students intuitively use AI like this. Maybe more will adopt this approach as they gain experience. But I also believe we should be teaching this skill. Students have access to AI regardless. A decent fraction of them will use it whether we want them to or not. We should coach them to use it as a learning resource rather than as an answer machine.

We can also talk about the fact that AI allows students to write *more* code and also write it *faster*. This can be an opportunity to assign more ambitious -- and, dare I say, more interesting -- projects. 

We lose the excitement of writing code with our own hands. But we gain the excitement of writing code that is more exciting than `print("Hello World!")`

These (in my opinion) are some areas to lean into:

- System design. Big-picture familiarity with the different components in a distributed system, as well as the constraints of thost components
- Verification. Static analysis, test-driven development, and validation criteria. Find problems fast to iterate fast
- Code defense. I don't care where you code came from. I want you to stand up and convince me that it's safe to deploy for a billion users
- DevOps concepts. AI can change thousands of lines of code in an instant. Agile/Scrum for scoping and coordination across a team. Pipelines, containers, and feature flags govern deployment. Telemetry, alerts, and oncall rotations are how you respond to problems that slip through
- Tech debt. Figuring out a legacy code base. Consolidating duplicate logic. Data and logic migrations
- Privacy and security. Responsible handling of user data. Vulnerabilities. Attack vectors
- Practice using AI tools! But in a way that doesn't short-circuit learning
