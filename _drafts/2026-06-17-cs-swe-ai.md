When I graduated from St Olaf College in 2009, there were about a dozen computer science majors per year. Fifteen years later, I came back to teach. The school overall remained about the same size, but the computer science department was now pumping out *sixty* majors per year. The major became much more popular because coding skills led to lucrative jobs in the fast-growing software engineering industry. 

Now the pendulum is swinging the other way. Recent advances in AI tooling have fundamentally changed what software engineering looks like. Line-by-line coding is mostly delegated to AI agents. Coding skills are much less marketable than they were a few years ago. Computer science educators are scrambling to adjust. 

I don't know the future, of course, but I've got a pretty good view of the present. I've been a software engineer for ten years. The past five have been in big tech. I also moonlight as a computer science professor. At first I dragged my feet on AI at work, but earlier this year it became too impactful to deny. Here's how things look from where I'm standing. 

## Demand for Coding Skills Peaked with the Pandemic

Over the course of the 2010s, the internet became a normal part of daily life. Tech companies hired aggressively as they expanded into brick-and-mortar markets. For example:

- Netflix [bankrupted Blockbuster][netflix_blockbuster] in 2010
- By 2014, more people got their [news from Facebook][fb_overtakes_newspapers] than from newspapers
- Rideshare apps [surpassed traditional taxis][uber_taxis] around 2015
- Music streaming apps [overtook music purchases][spotify_cds] around 2016

Then came the COVID pandemic. People were home all day glued to their smartphones. This was more money in tech company pockets, which further fueled the demand for software engineers:

- [Ad spending on social media][covid_online_ads] saw double-digit growth at the expense of print media
- App-based food delivery [tripled][covid_food_delivery]
- Video call apps saw usage increase [*thirty*-fold][covid_video_calls]. 

There were only so many software engineers to go around. Companies competed for them. Salaries and [perks][perks] went up. Hiring standards went down. I was lucky enough to experience this firsthand. A recruiter from Meta called me in late 2020. My resume was decent. My interview went badly. They ghosted me. But then, a few weeks later, their hiring quota went up. They called me back with an offer to double my salary.

College students took notice. They learned to code. From 2010 to 2024, the number of students graduating with a computer science major [increased five-fold][fivefold_increase]. The total number of software engineers in the USA, in turn, went from [one million in 2010][2010_swe_count] to [nearly two million in 2024][2024_swe_count]. 

But as the pandemic wound down, so did the hiring frenzy. [Software ate the whole world][software_ate_the_world], so now there's no more room to grow. Job postings have been [trending down][swe_jobs_dropping_post_covid] for the past few years. Layoffs are [rising][2026_tech_layoffs]. Salaries for new computer science grads are [dropping][swe_salary_dropping] closer to the other sciences. Majoring in computer science was still perfectly reasonable in 2024, but it was no longer a golden ticket.

People often study computer science to get hired as software engineers, but computer science and software engineering aren't quite the same thing:

- Computer science covers the fundamental logic, mathematics, and physics of computing. Computer science students learn how the machine works, algorithms for breaking down complex problems into machine-friendly steps, and programming languages for communicating those steps to the machine. 
- Software engineering is about delivering a product. Messaging platforms, food delivery apps, media players, operating systems, mobile games, etc. The user experience is what's important. The computer is just a tool along the way.

Your average software engineer doesn't need to worry about how compilers work. Most computer science grads don't really understand tech debt.

To be clear: software engineering is still a technical job! Software engineers just act more like tech leads now, rather than individual contributors. We spend a lot of time breaking down big goals into little tasks. We check the AI output for mistakes, and tweak the environment to help it do better next time. We keep track of how our product fits into the bigger system. These are traditionally senior-level skills, but they're now [being asked of junior engineers][senior_skills] too. If you believe that AI is here to stay then it might make sense to emphasize these skills more in school.

## AI Is Bigger than Coding

we do use it to write code. create new programs, modify existing programs. but we also (increasingly) use it as a way to interface with the machine more generally. AI streamlines all sorts of fiddly tasks. stuff that you don't really want to do by hand, but that's too specific to justfy writing a program to do it automatically. stuff that requires just a *little* bit of judgment. the sort of stuff that might previously have been delegated to a junior engineer or an intern:

- foo
- bar




AI isn't just for writing code. Software engineers use it for all sorts of tasks that could previously have been delegated to a junior engineer or intern. For example:

- Turning an informal prompt into a nice-looking document
- Parsing information from a bunch of different docs, turning it into a flowchart, and making note of conflicts
- Taking a bunch of old data and converting it to a more convenient format
- Researching the answer to a vague question
- Building a new dashboard to show metrics that only matter to a handful of people
- Summarizing project updates that happened while you were on vacation

For the most part, these use cases are not specific to software engineering. There are plenty of interns doing similar work in finance, retail, architecture, and a hundred other industries.

These skills are not specific to software engineering! There are plenty of interns in finance, retail, architecture, and a hundred other industries. That means there are plenty of menial tasks that can be delegated to AI agents. Software engineering was just the first industry to be affected, which, honestly, makes perfect sense:

- Tech had a head start. AI is closely related to machine learning; this is the technology that determines what shows up on your Facebook feed. AI is also closely related to natural language processing, which is the basis for assistant apps like Siri and Alexa
- Software engineering has tools to mitigate non-deterministic behavior. An AI agent can run static code analysis on its own output, see the mistakes, and iterate on them independently
- Tool integrations are easy in software engineering. We love writing scripts to automate the tedious parts of our own jobs. Pretty much everything we do is accessible via the Linux terminal. Once we got the AI agents plugged into the terminal, we were good to go
- Programming code is consumed by machines, not humans. As much as it pains me to say so, there is generally no punishment for ugly code, and generally no reward for beautiful code

Other industries will catch up. Wrappers and integration points will pop up to allow AI to interface nicely with proprietary software and data formats. Job responsibilities will change as we find that AI is undeniably better at some sorts of work. This will be a mix of good and bad, just as we've seen in software engineering. Outsourcing some work to AI will allow people to focus more on what they want to *do* and less on the tooling necessary to do it. The transition will also be a huge pain in the ass that leaves some folks behind. 

In the end, I think we'll see people across industries see computers much like software engineers do. To paraphrase [Alex][alex]: before you learn to code, you can only use a computer to solve problems that someone else has already considered. After you learn to \[vibe\]code, you can use a computer for anything.

## Abstinence-Only Education Doesn't Work

Researchers in China recently [published a study][china_ai_study] looking at how AI use impacted school performance. The top-line numbers are about what you would expect: the average student is getting better scores on homework, spending less time on it, and performing worse on tests. In other words, they're outsourcing their homework to AI, engaging less with the material, and learning less themselves. 

But some students defied the average. Some AI users spent just as much time on homework as their non-AI-user peers. These students say their test scores *increase* instead of decrease. The idea is that those students are using AI as a tutor, rather than as a machine that spits out the answers.

[china_ai_study]: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6868618

This aligns pretty closely with my experience using AI for curriculum development. AI can write slides just fine, but if I show up to class with AI-written slides I'm going to have a bad time. The voice will be off, emphasis might not line up with my priorities, and it might even bring up a topic I'm not prepared to talk about. But AI is an incredible resource for exploring new material. It can find answers even when I'm not sure exactly what I'm looking for. Chatting with an agent is both faster and more engaging than reading Wikipedia pages from top to bottom. For example:

- "I'm developing a lecture about the TCP/IP model of networking for an undergraduate computer science course. I'd like to talk about security. Can you list some important network vulnerabilities for each layer?"
- "My chapter on CPU architecture is feeling a bit light. We already cover the ALU, instruction pipelining, and hazards. Could you suggest some other topics I could add to flesh it out?"
- lorem ipsum...

The study above suggests that some students intuitively use AI like this. Maybe more will adopt this approach as they gain experience. But I also believe we should be teaching this skill. Students have access to AI regardless. A decent fraction of them will use it whether we want them to or not. We should coach them to use it as a learning resource rather than as an answer machine.

We can also talk about the fact that AI allows students to write *more* code and also write it *faster*. This can be an opportunity to assign more ambitious -- and, dare I say, more interesting -- projects. 

We lose the excitement of writing code with our own hands. But we gain the excitement of writing code that is more exciting than `print("Hello World!")`

These (in my opinion) are some areas to lean into:

- System design. Big-picture familiarity with the different components in a distributed system, as well as the constraints of thost components
- Verification. Static analysis, test-driven development, and validation criteria. Find problems fast to iterate fast
- Code defense. I don't care where you code came from. I want you to stand up and convince me that it's safe to deploy for a billion users
- DevOps concepts. AI can change thousands of lines of code in an instant. Agile/Scrum for scoping and coordination across a team. Pipelines, containers, and feature flags govern deployment. Telemetry, alerts, and oncall rotations are how you respond to problems that slip through
- Tech debt. Figuring out a legacy code base. Consolidating duplicate logic. Data and logic migrations
- Privacy and security. Responsible handling of user data. Vulnerabilities. Attack vectors
- Practice using AI tools! But in a way that doesn't short-circuit learning

- Gell-Mann amnesia effect

- We have to do away with "the bogus notion that there is a correct answer to this problem"
- We never cared about the algorithms knowledge from Leetcode problems. it was valuable as a signal for "I have invested extra time in gaining expertise here"

- Concern: how do we make sure we are not throwing students in the deep end? There is some pride that comes with building your first Hello World. Does that carry over if we jump straight into bigger AI-build projects?
- S-type work vs E-type work. Specified vs evolved. School mostly focuses on S-type work. Most "real" work is E-type, where you dig down until you can find some S-type stuff (then build a tool to solve it)
